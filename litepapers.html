Overview

Since 2014, stablecoins have proven themselves as the ideal mechanism for transferring value without exposing users to the price volatility of free-floating cryptocurrencies. Yet despite processing trillions in transaction volume and generating billions in revenue, the current stablecoin infrastructure operates on a fundamentally broken economic model that extracts value from users rather than sharing it with them.

Traditional stablecoins like Tether and USDC retain all revenues for their shareholders while providing users nothing beyond basic stability. When you hold $1000 in USDC, Circle earns yield on your capital while you receive zero participation in the value your deposits create. When stablecoin prices deviate and arbitrage opportunities emerge, whitelisted arbitrageurs capture these profits while users absorb the risk.

Even existing yield-bearing stablecoins fail to address this fundamental value extraction problem. While they redistribute small portions of underlying yields to users through permissioned mechanisms, they fail to solve the critical issue of mercenary liquidity, where users continuously chase higher returns elsewhere without building sustainable ecosystem value.

Adaptive Supply Cap and Revenue-Gated Issuance

The total maximum supply of BOUND ZERO is capped at 10 billion tokens, but this does not mean that all 10 billion tokens will necessarily enter circulation. Unlike traditional token models, where the full supply is pre-allocated or scheduled to be released over time regardless of usage or demand, BOUND ZERO uses a revenue-gated issuance model. In this system, tokens are only minted when the protocol generates actual income. That income must first pass through an on-chain allocation mechanism, which determines how much of it can be used to issue new BOUND ZERO tokens.

Even more importantly, the community of BOUND ZERO holders governs the emission logic. Through on-chain voting, the DAO can choose to reduce or completely halt token issuance regardless of the unused supply remaining in the emission pool. For instance, if the market price of BOUND ZERO is underperforming, or if the circulating supply is considered too high, the community may vote to allocate 100% of protocol revenue to token burns rather than continuing to mint and distribute new BOUND ZERO.

As a result, large portions of the 10 billion cap may remain permanently unminted, effectively reducing the long-term total supply. This introduces an adaptive supply ceiling that responds to market and governance conditions while preserving the protocol's economic sustainability. In essence, the 10 billion token cap is a hard upper limit, not a supply target and the actual circulating supply may be significantly lower, based entirely on real usage, governance decisions, and protocol performance.
